Research & Theories

Katherine Johnson was a mathematician whose precise calculations defined the trajectories of America's most iconic space missions, such as those of Alan Shepard and Apollo 11. Her work, blending mathematical rigor and innovation*, established solid foundations for space navigation. This page presents her main research, methods, and theories, essential for understanding her fundamental role in space conquest history.

Work Accomplished

Katherine Johnson performed essential mathematical calculations for navigation and safety of American space flights. In 1960, she co-authored a report describing orbital trajectories*, a first for a woman at NASA. Her work included precise determination of launch windows, capsule trajectories in orbit, and emergency plans in case of unforeseen events.

She also manually verified calculations produced by early computers, notably for the historic Mercury-Atlas 6 mission with astronaut John Glenn. Her expertise in applied mathematics enabled complex space rendezvous like Apollo 11's between the lunar module and command module, ensuring safe and precise maneuvers.

Impact & Legacy

Katherine Johnson's impact goes far beyond her calculations. By ensuring precision and safety of space missions, she played a decisive role in America's victory in the space race*. More than a mathematician, she became a symbol of perseverance against racial and sexist barriers, inspiring future generations to embrace STEM careers.

Her legacy continues through institutional and popular recognition, with honors such as the Presidential Medal of Freedom and NASA buildings named after her. The book and film "Hidden Figures" popularized her story, making her a universal model of success and equality. Her work continues to influence contemporary aerospace science, reminding that scientific progress is as human as it is technical.
